🌿 Dendrites - The Input Receivers

Structure

Branch-like extensions from the cell body, like tree branches. Can have thousands of dendritic spines.

Function

Receive neurotransmitter signals from other neurons and convert them to electrical signals.

Signal Processing

Integrate multiple inputs through temporal and spatial summation before sending to soma.

🔴 Cell Body (Soma) - The Control Center

Contains

Nucleus, mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, and other organelles.

Function

Metabolic center, integrates incoming signals, and decides whether to generate action potential.

Size Range

Typically 5-100 micrometers in diameter, varying by neuron type and function.

🟢 Nucleus - The Genetic Command Center

Contains: DNA, nucleolus, nuclear envelope with pores

Functions: Gene expression control, protein synthesis regulation, cell division control

Clinical Relevance: Nuclear dysfunction linked to neurodegenerative diseases

🔵 Axon Hillock - The Decision Point

Location: Junction between cell body and axon

Function: Site of action potential initiation - where electrical threshold is reached

Mechanism: High concentration of voltage-gated sodium channels

Threshold: Typically -55mV in mammalian neurons

🔵 Axon - The Information Highway

Structure

Long projection from cell body. Can be millimeters to over a meter long in humans.

Function

Conducts action potentials away from cell body at speeds up to 120 m/s.

Specializations

Contains specialized ion channels, cytoskeleton, and transport mechanisms.

🟡 Myelin Sheath - The Speed Booster

Composition: 70% lipids, 30% proteins - primarily phospholipids and cholesterol

Formation: Oligodendrocytes (CNS) and Schwann cells (PNS) wrap around axons

Function: Increases conduction velocity by 10-100 times through saltatory conduction

Clinical Significance: Demyelinating diseases like Multiple Sclerosis affect signal transmission

🔴 Nodes of Ranvier - The Signal Boosters

Structure: Gaps in myelin sheath every 1-2mm along axon

Function: Sites of action potential regeneration during saltatory conduction

Ion Channels: High density of voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels

Speed Advantage: Allows signal to "jump" between nodes at 120 m/s vs 1-2 m/s in unmyelinated axons

🌱 Axon Terminals - The Signal Transmitters

Structure

Bulbous endings containing synaptic vesicles filled with neurotransmitters.

Function

Convert electrical signals to chemical signals through neurotransmitter release.

Vesicle Types

Small clear vesicles (classical NTs) and large dense-core vesicles (neuropeptides).

🟠 Synapses - The Communication Junctions

Types: Chemical synapses (most common) and electrical synapses (gap junctions)

Components: Presynaptic terminal, synaptic cleft (20-40nm), postsynaptic membrane

Neurotransmitters: Acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, GABA, glutamate, and many others

Plasticity: Synaptic strength can change - basis of learning and memory

Number: Average neuron has 1,000-10,000 synaptic connections

🧠 Gray Matter

Contains:

  • Neuronal cell bodies (soma)
  • Dendrites and dendritic spines
  • Unmyelinated axons
  • Synaptic connections
  • Glial cells (astrocytes, microglia)
  • Blood capillaries

Function: Information processing, integration, and computation

Location: Cerebral cortex, basal ganglia, thalamic nuclei

Appearance: Darker due to cell bodies and blood vessels

🤍 White Matter

Contains:

  • Myelinated axon tracts
  • Oligodendrocytes (myelin-producing cells)
  • Few cell bodies
  • Astrocytes and microglia
  • Blood vessels

Function: Rapid information transmission between brain regions

Location: Corpus callosum, internal capsule, brain stem tracts

Appearance: Lighter due to fatty myelin sheaths
